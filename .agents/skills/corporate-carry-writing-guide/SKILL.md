# Corporate Carry Writing Guide

Use this skill to produce Corporate Carry editorial content that helps readers carry less, choose better, and avoid workday failure.

Corporate Carry is an editorial guide to the objects that make modern professional work easier: gadgets, tools, backpack essentials, desk upgrades, travel kit, meeting gear, notebooks, pens, and professional gift guides for office workers, hybrid workers, consultants, travelers, and people who live between desks, conference rooms, airports, and Slack threads.

The voice is **practical, selective, and quietly opinionated**. It should sound like experienced professionals who have actually carried, packed, used, forgotten, regretted, returned, and re-bought the items being discussed.

## When To Use

Use this skill for:

- Individual product reviews
- Item-centric review pages
- Roundups and tiered buying guides
- Feature articles and testing narratives
- Category landing page copy
- Newsletter issues for The Friday Carry
- Product card blurbs
- Headlines, decks, meta descriptions, captions, and excerpts
- Editorial QA against the Corporate Carry voice
- Jekyll/Liquid post, item, and category front matter for editorial content

If the task is primarily visual UI design, also use the `corporate-carry-design` skill.

## Operating Model

Before writing, identify the content type and the decision the reader needs to make.

1. **Clarify the job of the piece**
   - Is this a product review, roundup, feature, category page, newsletter, product card, or rewrite?
   - What workday problem is being solved?
   - Who is the reader: hybrid worker, consultant, traveler, operator, remote worker, or office regular?
   - What should the reader know to buy, skip, remove, or compare?

2. **Anchor the piece in use**
   - Use testing context, realistic work scenarios, weights, prices, capacities, battery life, dimensions, and failure modes.
   - Translate specs into consequences for office days, commutes, client visits, hotel desks, conference rooms, and airport days.
   - If details are missing, label assumptions or ask up to three focused questions.

3. **Lead with judgment**
   - Start with the verdict, not throat-clearing.
   - Make the use case concrete.
   - Name who should skip it.
   - Include tradeoffs.

4. **Write in the house voice**
   - Plain English.
   - Short, confident paragraphs.
   - Specific nouns.
   - Light dry humor, never performance.
   - Clear editorial independence.
   - No hype.

5. **Finish with a quality check**
   - Confirm the piece has a verdict.
   - Confirm the audience is specific.
   - Confirm specs are translated into workday impact.
   - Confirm tradeoffs and failure modes are named.
   - Confirm no section sounds like retailer copy.

## Core Editorial Promise

Every Corporate Carry article should do at least three of these:

1. Reduce clutter.
2. Save time.
3. Prevent workday failure.
4. Improve professional readiness.
5. Make buying decisions easier.
6. Explain what not to buy.
7. Show what actually survives repeated use.

The editorial mantra is: **Earn the spot.**

A product earns the spot by solving a recurring problem, surviving repeated use, reducing clutter, and making the workday easier without demanding attention.

## Voice Rules

### Do

Use:

- Plain English
- Specific nouns
- Short, confident sentences
- Lightly dry humor
- Real-life work scenarios
- Measurable details
- Clear judgments
- We tested, we kept, we returned, we removed
- Earns its spot, survived the week, stayed in the bag, back to the drawer

### Do Not

Avoid:

- Breathless hype
- Fake urgency
- Influencer language
- Luxury-magazine aspiration
- Corporate-brochure blandness
- Retailer copy
- Long SEO throat-clearing
- Padding lists for search

Do not use these unless quoting or explicitly critiquing them:

- Game-changer
- Must-have
- Ultimate
- Elevate
- Crush your workflow
- Premium lifestyle
- Perfect for everyone
- Everyone needs this
- We’re obsessed
- This changed everything

## Audience

Write for professionals who care about competence more than gear culture.

Primary readers include:

- Hybrid office workers
- Consultants and client-facing professionals
- Product managers, designers, engineers, executives, and operators
- Business travelers
- Remote workers with occasional office days
- People who want a cleaner, lighter, more dependable work setup

Assume the reader is busy, skeptical, and allergic to filler. Their hidden question is:

> Will this make my workday easier, or will it become another thing I manage?

## House Style

- Use sentence case for headlines.
- Use numerals for measurements, prices, scores, and item counts.
- Use metric weights where possible: g, kg, L.
- Include USD prices unless covering another market.
- Use laptop, not notebook computer.
- Use work bag or corporate carry, not EDC loadout unless speaking to enthusiast context.
- Use hybrid worker, office day, client visit, commute, hotel desk, and conference room as grounding language.
- Use contractions where natural.
- Prefer active voice.
- Use em dashes sparingly.
- Use bullets for kits, specs, and comparison points.
- Include testing dates when possible.
- Include last updated dates on evergreen pages.
- Keep product names consistent with `product-list.md` or canonical `_items` records.

## Product Review Structure

Use this for individual item-centric reviews.

1. **Verdict**
   - Start with the bottom line.
   - Example: The best small charger for hybrid workers who carry one laptop, one phone, and no patience for cable sprawl.

2. **Best for**
   - Name the reader and use case.
   - Example: Best for office days, client visits, train commutes, and anyone replacing a drawer of old charging bricks.

3. **What it solves**
   - Explain the workday problem.
   - Name the failure mode it prevents.

4. **What we tested**
   - Include context, duration, and conditions.
   - Example: Tested for six weeks across office days, two flights, hotel desks, and one conference room with a single hostile outlet.

5. **What worked**
   - Use concrete observations.
   - Translate specs into behavior.

6. **What did not**
   - Every real review needs friction.
   - Name the drawback without drama.

7. **Who should skip it**
   - Help the reader opt out.
   - Narrow the recommendation.

8. **Final call**
   - End with judgment.
   - Example: Not the most powerful charger we tested. The one most editors kept packed.

## Roundup Structure

Roundups should be selective, not bloated.

1. **Opening thesis**
   - What problem is this roundup solving?
   - What did you exclude?

2. **Selection criteria**
   - Weight
   - Price
   - Frequency of use
   - Failure modes
   - Compatibility
   - Desk, office, commute, travel, or client-site fit

3. **Tier 1: Buy these first**
   - 3-5 items maximum.
   - These are the items that earn a spot for most readers.

4. **Tier 2: Worth it for specific roles**
   - Useful, but not universal.

5. **Skip / not for most people**
   - Products that are tempting but unnecessary.

6. **Final kit recommendation**
   - The smallest useful set.

Good roundup titles:

- The 7 backpack essentials that survived a real hybrid workweek
- Desk upgrades we kept after the novelty wore off

Avoid:

- 25 amazing office gadgets every professional needs right now

## Feature Article Structure

Use for long-form testing pieces, gear systems, travel setups, and editorial essays.

1. **Headline**
   - Make the subject and test clear.
   - Example: The hybrid worker’s backpack setup, tested across 14 cities.

2. **Deck**
   - State what was learned and why it matters.

3. **Opening**
   - Start with the practical tension.
   - Example: Most people buy a bag for their worst day and carry it half-empty on every normal one.

4. **Method**
   - Describe the test plainly.
   - Use testing duration, conditions, tracking method, and removal criteria.

5. **Findings**
   - Organize by decisions, not products.
   - Example findings: buy for the average day; organization beats capacity; power belongs in one pouch; the second version of anything is usually clutter.

6. **Final kit**
   - End with a clear loadout, including weights and prices where relevant.

7. **What we removed**
   - Always include what failed to earn space.
   - This is one of the most Corporate Carry sections an article can have.

## Category Page Structure

Category pages should act like opinionated buying maps.

Include:

- Plain-language category definition
- Who the category is for
- Number of items reviewed
- Editor’s picks
- Average price
- Last updated date
- Editorial note
- Use-case filters
- Tiered recommendations
- Quick comparison table
- Adjacent categories

Category copy formula:

> These are the [category] items that solve [specific workday problem]. We tested for [conditions]. We kept the ones that [selection standard] and removed the ones that [failure standard].

Example:

> These are the desk upgrades that make a workday smoother without turning your desk into a gadget museum. We tested for setup time, daily use, cable mess, and whether the item still felt useful after week three.

## Newsletter Structure: The Friday Carry

The Friday Carry should be brief, useful, and decisive.

Use this structure:

1. **Subject line**
   - Example: The one cable we stopped stealing from each other.

2. **Opening**
   - One sentence on the problem.

3. **The pick**
   - Name the item and why it matters.

4. **Why it stayed**
   - Explain the real-world use.

5. **Who should skip it**
   - Keep trust by narrowing the audience.

6. **One-line verdict**
   - Example: Boring, reliable, and now permanently in the meeting pouch.

## Product Blurb Formula

Product blurbs should be compact, concrete, and opinionated.

Formula:

> [Problem solved]. [Key proof/spec]. [Human workday detail].

Example:

> Replaces the charger pile with one 95 g brick. Handles a laptop, phone, and earbuds at once. Small enough to forget until the day you need it.

Product card guidance:

- Product name
- Category
- Primary use case
- Price
- Weight or relevant spec
- Where it fits in the carry
- Verdict
- One limitation

## Category-Specific Review Criteria

### Backpacks and Bags

Evaluate:

- Laptop protection
- Comfort under realistic load
- Access while commuting
- Professional appearance
- Weather resistance
- Size discipline
- Whether it stands up, sags, or sprawls
- Whether it fits under a desk, train seat, or conference-room chair

### Chargers and Power

Evaluate:

- Real laptop compatibility
- Port layout
- Heat
- Weight
- Cable pairing
- Travel usefulness
- Whether it reduces total kit weight

### Pouches and Organization

Evaluate:

- Opening style
- Pocket logic
- Visibility
- Over-organization risk
- Desk footprint
- Whether items return to the same place naturally

### Desk Upgrades

Evaluate:

- Setup time
- Cable impact
- Daily use frequency
- Ergonomic benefit
- Whether it still earns space after three weeks

### Meeting Gear

Evaluate:

- Reliability under pressure
- Setup speed
- Compatibility
- Professional discretion
- Whether it prevents awkwardness in front of clients

### Travel Kit

Evaluate:

- Weight
- Failure prevention
- Airport/hotel usefulness
- International compatibility
- Whether it saves time or just feels prepared

### Pens and Notebooks

Evaluate:

- Comfort
- Refill reliability
- Paper behavior
- Meeting appropriateness
- Whether it gets used instead of admired

## Scoring Guidance

Scores should be rare, explained, and defensible.

Use a 10-point scale only when the review includes enough testing to justify it.

- **9.0-10:** Editor’s pick. Stayed in rotation. Easy to recommend.
- **8.0-8.9:** Strong, but role-specific or with clear tradeoffs.
- **7.0-7.9:** Good product, not essential.
- **6.0-6.9:** Useful for narrow cases; most readers can skip.
- **Below 6:** Do not recommend unless there is a very specific reason.

Never give a high score without naming the tradeoff.

## Independence and Trust

Corporate Carry should sound allergic to pay-for-play content.

Required habits:

- Say how the product was obtained when relevant.
- Separate editorial judgment from availability.
- Do not let retailer copy influence review language.
- Do not recommend a product solely because it is new.
- Do not use manufacturer claims unless tested or clearly attributed.
- Do not hide downsides.
- Do not pad lists for SEO.

Approved trust-building phrases:

- We tested...
- We kept...
- We removed...
- It did not earn space...
- Good, but not for most people...
- The cheaper option is enough...
- Skip it unless...

## SEO Without Sounding Like SEO

Use natural keywords inside useful prose.

Good keyword patterns:

- best backpack essentials for hybrid work
- compact charger for office backpack
- business travel tech pouch
- consultant laptop bag setup
- desk gadgets that improve productivity

SEO title formula:

> [Specific product/category] for [specific professional use case], tested in [real condition]

Meta description formula:

> We tested [category] across [conditions] to find the [number] items that actually earn space in a professional work bag.

Never keyword-stuff.

## Jekyll Front Matter Guidance

When generating Corporate Carry content for Jekyll, include front matter that supports item-centric relationships.

### Product Review Post

Use fields such as:

```yaml
---
layout: post
title: "[Product/category] for [specific professional use case], tested in [real condition]"
date: YYYY-MM-DD
content_type: review
dek: "[One-sentence practical promise.]"
author: maya-reyes
primary_item: item-slug
items:
  - item-slug
pillars:
  - pillar-slug
tags:
  - tag
read_time: "X min"
testing:
  duration: "X weeks"
  conditions:
    - office days
    - client visits
scores:
  overall: 9.1
related_items:
  - item-slug
related_posts:
  - post-slug
---
```

### Item Record

Use fields such as:

```yaml
---
layout: item
title: "[Canonical product name]"
brand: "[Fictional brand]"
product_line: "[Product line]"
model: "[Model]"
item_type: "[charger | pouch | backpack | notebook | pen | etc.]"

  - pillar-slug

  - name: Meridian Market
  - name: Voltforge

  placeholder_label: "product · charger"

  - item-slug

  - item-slug

```

Use fields such as:

```yaml

  - item-slug

  - pillar-slug

```

## Image and Caption Guidance

Corporate Carry imagery should feel documented, not staged to death.

Preferred image types:

- Flat lays of real kits
- Bag contents after testing
- Desk setups after a workweek
- Pouches open and loaded
- Scale/weight shots
- Conference-room or hotel-desk context
- Before/after organization shots

Captions should explain what the reader is seeing and why it matters.

Good:

> The final seven-item kit after 278 days: charger, cable, hub, headphones, notebook, pen, and tracker.

Weak:

> A beautiful arrangement of premium workday essentials.

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing Corporate Carry copy, confirm:

- The article has a clear verdict.
- The audience is specific.
- The recommendation is based on use, not vibes.
- Tradeoffs are named.
- At least one item was rejected, skipped, or narrowed.
- Specs are translated into workday impact.
- No section sounds like retailer copy.
- The headline is useful without hype.
- The opening gets to the point within two sentences.
- The piece helps the reader carry less, choose better, or avoid a mistake.
- Product names match `product-list.md` or canonical `_items` records.
- If scoring is used, the score is supported by testing and a named tradeoff.

## Rewrite Checklist

When rewriting existing copy:

1. Identify the weak claim, hype phrase, vague recommendation, or SEO filler.
2. Replace it with a concrete workday problem.
3. Add proof: price, weight, duration, use condition, failure mode, or tested result.
4. Name the audience and tradeoff.
5. Remove language that sounds like retailer copy.

Example transformation:

Before:

> This premium tech pouch is the ultimate solution for professionals who want to elevate their everyday organization.

After:

> This is the pouch for people whose cables have started forming governments. It opens flat, holds the charger stack, and makes the one cable you need visible before the meeting starts.
